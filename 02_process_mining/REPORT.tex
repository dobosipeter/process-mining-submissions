\section{Introduction and Goal}

This report presents a process mining analysis of two event logs from the BPI Challenge 2020 dataset, which records the travel expense reimbursement process at Eindhoven University of Technology (TU/e).

The central question driving this analysis is one posed by the challenge organizers themselves:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Is there a difference in throughput between domestic and international travel declarations?}
\end{quote}

This question resonated with my own experience: through my employment, I have first-hand familiarity with the bureaucracy surrounding international travel administration — the permit approvals, budget owner sign-offs, and multi-step authorization chains — and how much more involved it is compared to domestic travel, which in practice proceeds almost automatically. This motivated me to investigate whether the data confirms that intuition quantitatively.

To answer this, the analysis pursues the following objectives:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Compare throughput} — measure and compare case durations (from submission to payment) between domestic and international declarations, both overall and at each approval step.
    \item \textbf{Identify bottlenecks} — determine where in the approval workflow the longest delays occur, and whether they differ between the two declaration types.
    \item \textbf{Discover and compare process models} — apply multiple discovery algorithms to both logs and evaluate the resulting models using conformance checking metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Dataset Description}

\subsection{Source}

The dataset originates from the \textbf{BPI Challenge 2020}, the tenth International Business Process Intelligence Challenge organized by the IEEE Task Force on Process Mining. The data captures the travel expense reimbursement process at TU/e, covering 2017 (two departments) and 2018 (university-wide). It is publicly available at \url{https://data.4tu.nl/collections/BPI_Challenge_2020/5065541/1}.

\subsection{Process Overview}

At TU/e, domestic and international trips follow different procedures:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Domestic trips:} No prior permission is needed. The employee travels and submits a reimbursement claim afterwards.
    \item \textbf{International trips:} A travel permit must be approved by the supervisor before making any arrangements. Pre-paid travel costs can be claimed before the trip; the full declaration is submitted after the trip.
\end{itemize}

Both declaration types follow a similar approval workflow once submitted:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Submission by the employee.
    \item Approval by travel administration.
    \item Forwarding to the budget owner.
    \item Forwarding to the supervisor (skipped if the budget owner and supervisor are the same person).
    \item Optional director approval.
    \item Payment request and execution.
\end{enumerate}

At any approval step, a rejection can occur. The employee may then resubmit or withdraw the request.

The notably higher events-per-case ratio for international declarations (11.2 vs.\ 5.4) already suggests a more complex process with more steps or more rework. The international log contains nearly double the number of distinct activities (34 vs.\ 17) because it includes the travel permit approval workflow (e.g.\ \texttt{Permit SUBMITTED by EMPLOYEE}, \texttt{Permit APPROVED by ADMINISTRATION}) as well as trip lifecycle events (\texttt{Start trip}, \texttt{End trip}) and reminders.

Both logs record only two resource identifiers: \texttt{STAFF MEMBER} and \texttt{SYSTEM}. However, the functional role of each actor is captured in the \texttt{org:role} attribute (e.g.\ \texttt{EMPLOYEE}, \texttt{ADMINISTRATION}, \texttt{BUDGET OWNER}, \texttt{SUPERVISOR}).

Each event carries the following key attributes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \texttt{concept:name} — the activity label.
    \item \texttt{time:timestamp} — the timestamp of the event.
    \item \texttt{org:role} — the functional role of the actor (e.g.\ EMPLOYEE, SUPERVISOR).
    \item \texttt{case:Amount} — the declared amount (anonymized but proportionally preserved).
    \item \texttt{case:BudgetNumber} — the budget line charged.
\end{itemize}
The international log additionally includes permit-related case attributes such as \texttt{case:Permit\-RequestedBudget} and \texttt{case:Permit\-OrganizationalEntity}.

\section{Data Preparation}

The BPI 2020 declaration logs do not include a \texttt{lifecycle:transition} attribute. Each event already represents a single completed action, so no lifecycle filtering is necessary.

Both logs were inspected for missing values — none were found in any column.

No activities were excluded or merged. The international log's permit and trip events (\texttt{Permit SUBMITTED}, \texttt{Start trip}, etc.) are kept in the analysis because they are part of the actual process that an international declaration goes through. Removing them would mask the true complexity difference between the two declaration types.

The only preparation step applied was formatting via \texttt{pm4py.format\_dataframe()} to map the standard XES attribute names (\texttt{case:concept:name}, \texttt{concept:name}, \texttt{time:timestamp}) to pm4py's expected column identifiers.

It is worth noting that this dataset is remarkably clean — no missing values, no ambiguous lifecycle transitions, and well-structured activity labels. This is characteristic of a curated challenge dataset. Real-world event logs would typically require more extensive preparation: handling missing or inconsistent timestamps, resolving duplicate events, dealing with incomplete cases, mapping free-text activity names to a consistent vocabulary, and filtering out noise from system-generated events.

\section{Process Discovery}

Three discovery algorithms were selected to cover a range of approaches and trade-offs:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Alpha Miner} — included as the foundational baseline algorithm to illustrate its limitations on real-world data.
    \item \textbf{Heuristic Miner} — chosen for its ability to handle noise and infrequent behavior via frequency thresholds, making it better suited to real-world logs.
    \item \textbf{Inductive Miner} — chosen for its soundness guarantees (deadlock-free models) and its ability to produce multiple representations (Petri net, process tree, BPMN).
\end{itemize}
Additionally, Directly-Follows Graphs (DFGs) are used as a lightweight exploratory visualization before applying the formal discovery algorithms.

\subsection{Directly-Follows Graph}

As a first step in process discovery, Directly-Follows Graphs (DFGs) were constructed for both logs. A DFG connects each pair of activities that directly follow each other in at least one case, annotated with either transition frequencies or average elapsed times.

The domestic DFG (Figures~\ref{fig:dom_dfg_freq} and~\ref{fig:dom_dfg_perf}) is compact and readable, reflecting the simple approval chain. The performance-annotated version reveals where the longest waits occur in the domestic process.

The international DFGs (Figures~\ref{fig:intl_dfg_freq} and~\ref{fig:intl_dfg_perf}) are significantly more complex — with 34 activities and many cross-connections, the graphs become difficult to interpret visually. This ``spaghetti'' effect is a well-known limitation of DFGs on complex processes and motivates the use of more sophisticated discovery algorithms that can abstract away infrequent behavior.

Edges that are traversed by only a few cases can show misleadingly high averages if those cases happen to be outliers. \subsection{Alpha Miner}

The Alpha Miner is included as a baseline. It discovers a Petri net by analyzing the ordering relations (causality, parallelism, choice) between activities directly from the log. It is the foundational process discovery algorithm, but it assumes a clean, noise-free log and cannot handle short loops, invisible tasks, or infrequent behavior.

Figure~\ref{fig:dom_alpha} shows the domestic Petri net. While the main approval chain is visible, the model is already cluttered by the rejection and resubmission paths. Figure~\ref{fig:intl_alpha} shows the international result — with 34 activities, the Alpha Miner produces a model that is effectively unreadable, reflecting its inability to abstract away noise and infrequent paths.

\subsection{Heuristic Miner}

The Heuristic Miner extends the Alpha Miner's approach by applying frequency thresholds: only activity relations that appear sufficiently often are included in the model, filtering out noise and infrequent behavior. This makes it more suitable for real-world logs where not every observed transition is meaningful.

Figure~\ref{fig:dom_heuristic} shows a visibly cleaner domestic model compared to the Alpha Miner's output — the main approval chain (submission $\rightarrow$ administration $\rightarrow$ budget owner $\rightarrow$ supervisor $\rightarrow$ payment) is clearly identifiable, with rejection paths branching off at each step. The international model (Figure~\ref{fig:intl_heuristic}) is still complex due to the inherent process complexity (34 activities), but shows more structure than the Alpha Miner's version, with the permit and declaration sub-workflows more distinguishable.

\subsection{Inductive Miner}

The Inductive Miner uses a divide-and-conquer strategy: it recursively partitions the log into sublogs and identifies the process operator (sequence, parallel, choice, loop) that best explains each partition. This guarantees a \emph{sound} model — one that is free of deadlocks — and typically achieves high fitness. The trade-off is that the model may be overly permissive (low precision), allowing behavior that was never observed, in order to maintain soundness.

The filled black rectangles visible in the Petri net figures are \emph{silent (invisible) transitions} — a standard Petri net element that represents routing logic with no corresponding activity in the log. The Heuristic and Inductive miners introduce them to handle choices, parallelism, and loops structurally.

The domestic Petri net (Figure~\ref{fig:dom_inductive_pn}), BPMN model (Figure~\ref{fig:dom_inductive_bpmn}), and process tree (Figure~\ref{fig:dom_inductive_tree}) clearly show the approval chain with choice points at each step — the BPMN representation is particularly readable, while the process tree reveals the hierarchical operator structure (sequence, choice, loop) that the Inductive Miner uses internally. The international Petri net (Figure~\ref{fig:intl_inductive_pn}), BPMN model (Figure~\ref{fig:intl_inductive_bpmn}), and process tree (Figure~\ref{fig:intl_inductive_tree}) are more complex but still structured, with the permit and declaration sub-workflows identifiable as separate regions.

\section{Conformance Checking and Model Comparison}

To compare the discovered models quantitatively, token-based replay was used to compute three quality metrics:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Fitness} — what fraction of the observed behavior can the model reproduce?
    \item \textbf{Precision} — does the model allow behavior not observed in the log? Low precision means the model is too permissive.
    \item \textbf{Generalization} — can the model generalize beyond the specific traces observed?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}

The \textbf{Alpha Miner} performs poorly on both logs. On the domestic log, fitness (0.72) and precision (0.25) are both low. On the international log, the situation is worse: fitness drops to 0.54 and precision falls to 0.00 — the model cannot meaningfully represent the observed behavior. This confirms that the Alpha Miner is unsuitable for real-world processes with this level of complexity.

The \textbf{Inductive Miner} achieves the highest fitness on both logs (1.00 domestic, 0.97 international), which is expected given its soundness guarantees. However, its precision is low (0.39 domestic, 0.23 international), meaning the models are overly permissive — they accept many traces that were never observed. This is the well-known fitness--precision trade-off of the Inductive Miner.

The \textbf{Heuristic Miner} again provides the best balance. On the domestic log, it achieves high fitness (0.93) and high precision (0.87). On the international log, it achieves 0.90 fitness with an even higher precision of 0.91 — the best precision of any model on either log. Its generalization is somewhat lower (0.68 international), suggesting the model fits the observed traces tightly, but this is an acceptable trade-off given the high precision.

\textbf{Conclusion:} For both logs, the Heuristic Miner produces the most useful model, offering the best balance of faithfulness and specificity. The frequency thresholds that distinguish it from the Alpha Miner are critical for filtering out the noise and infrequent paths that dominate real-world event logs.

\section{Evaluation and Conclusion}

Returning to the three objectives stated in the introduction:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Throughput comparison:} There is a clear and substantial throughput difference between domestic and international declarations. Domestic cases have a median duration of 7.3 days; international cases have a median of 66.0 days — roughly 9 times longer. The distributions barely overlap, confirming this is a systematic difference rather than a statistical artifact.

    \item \textbf{Bottleneck identification:} The duration decomposition revealed that the trip itself (median 4.0 days) accounts for only a small fraction of the international case duration. The dominant contributors are the pre-trip permit approval phase (median 26.4 days) and the post-trip declaration approval phase (median 19.7 days). Even after removing the trip duration entirely, international administrative overhead (median 58.3 days) remains roughly 8 times longer than the entire domestic process. The additional permit steps, multi-level authorization chains, and higher rejection/resubmission rates all contribute to this disparity.

    \item \textbf{Model comparison:} Three discovery algorithms were applied to both logs. The Alpha Miner produced unusable models (especially for the international log, where precision was 0.00). The Inductive Miner achieved the highest fitness but at the cost of low precision. The Heuristic Miner provided the best overall balance on both logs, with domestic scores of 0.93/0.87/0.75 and international scores of 0.90/0.91/0.68 (fitness/precision/generalization).
\end{enumerate}

To answer the central question: \textbf{yes}, there is a large difference in throughput between domestic and international travel declarations, and it is driven primarily by administrative overhead — not by the travel itself.
